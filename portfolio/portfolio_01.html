Underwater Virtual Reality for Neutral Buoyancy Training

This work has been peer-reviewed and published! You can find it at the link below: Underwater Virtual Reality for Neutral Buoyancy Training: Development and Evaluation.

Summary

Astronauts must train extensively for activities conducted in space on terrestrial facilities like the Neutral Buoyancy Lab at Johnson Space Center. These training facilities place replica spacecraft in a neutrally-buoyant pool, providing a high-fidelty training environment that also mimics the loss of non-visual sensory information experienced in space. Maintenance and use of these facilities is expensive, however, and here we explore a lower-cost alternative using virtual reality. We developed custom training software alongside a head-mounted display integrated into a full-face SCUBA dive mask, then successfully pilot test this system with untrained users both above water and in a neutrally buoyant underwater environment.

Roles: Project lead, hardware prototyper, lead data analyst, lead experimenter, lead author

Context

- Astronauts required to do lots of things.

- Occasionally, astronauts required to leave the space craft to conduct extra-vehicular activities

- To train for tasks like these that require operating in microgravity, astronauts train in environments that simulate weightlessness/altered sensory environment.

- Main facility for this type of training is the Neutral Buoyancy Lab at Johnson Space Center

Problem

- Training is acceptable, but prohibitively expensive

- NASA often rents facility out to external agencies

Research Goals

- Develop a low-cost alternative to current training methods

- Determine how usable such an alternative is.

Approach

An immediate low-cost alternative that came to mind was virtual reality. VR allows substantive control over visual information presented to users in the virtual environment. While users may be susceptible to VR or simulator sickness, this is largely thought to be driven by conflicts between visual and non-visual sensory information. In microgravity, many sources of non-visual information are rendered noisy or eliminated. Similarly, non-visual cues to movement are mostly removed in the underwater, neutrally buoyant environment.

Unfortunately, VR systems capable of working underwater for prolonged periods of time are not widely available. To that end, we designed and developed hardware that allowed us to integrate a smartphone into a commercially available full-face SCUBA diving mask.
